Symptoms

Recognizing the symptoms of coronary artery disease (CAD) is crucial for early detection and treatment, reducing the risk of severe cardiovascular events and improving patient outcomes. Awareness of these symptoms enables timely medical intervention, enhancing quality of life and potentially lowering healthcare costs.

Symptoms

A heart attack can be the first indication of CAD for some individuals. This occurs when a coronary artery becomes completely blocked, leading to symptoms like severe chest pain or pressure, pain in the shoulder or arm, shortness of breath, and sweating. However, some heart attacks can happen without noticeable signs or symptoms. Heart palpitations, feeling like the heart is pounding, racing, or skipping beats, may also occur. While infrequent and brief palpitations are often not a concern, persistent or severe palpitations should be evaluated. Occasionally, chest pain may be accompanied by vomiting, indigestion, or nausea, which can be mistaken for other medical issues such as acid reflux. Recognizing these symptoms is crucial for timely diagnosis and treatment of CAD.

The symptoms of coronary artery disease (CAD) can vary widely depending on the severity of the condition. Some individuals may not exhibit any symptoms, while others might experience a range of signs. One common symptom is chest pain, known as angina. This pain can feel like pressure or tightness, often described as having someone standing on your chest. Angina usually occurs in the middle or left side of the chest and may be triggered by physical activity or intense emotions. The discomfort typically subsides within minutes after the triggering event ends. In some cases, particularly in women, the pain can be brief and sharp, extending to the neck, arm, or back (Mayo Clinic, 2022). Another symptom is shortness of breath, which may accompany chest discomfort and give a sensation of not being able to catch your breath. Fatigue is also a common sign, indicating that the heart might not be pumping enough blood to meet the body's needs.
